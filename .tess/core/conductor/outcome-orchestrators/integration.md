# Outcome Orchestrator Integration Doctrine

**Purpose:** Cross-orchestrator routing discipline, ownership clarity, and structural integrity  
**Applies to:** All 6 Outcome Orchestrators and Tess's mission routing logic  
**Authority:** System-level — overrides any individual orchestrator's self-claimed scope where conflict exists

---

## Part 1 — Identified Overlaps and Routing Ambiguities

The following mission types create genuine ownership tension across orchestrators. Each has been resolved with an explicit ruling.

---

### Overlap 1 — Strategic Direction and Competitive Positioning
**Orchestrators in tension:** Founder's Office vs Strategic Growth

**The ambiguity:** Both claim "whole-business competitive positioning" and "strategic direction."

**Resolution:**
- **Founder's Office owns** competitive positioning when the question is: *what is our identity, our strategic intent, and our highest-leverage directional choice as a business?* This is positioning at the level of the whole business and the founder's priorities.
- **Strategic Growth owns** competitive positioning when the question is: *how do we differentiate and win in a specific market, vertical, or against a specific competitor set?* This is positioning as a tactical-strategic instrument for market-specific expansion.

**Rule:** If the positioning question affects the whole business identity → Founder's Office. If it affects a specific market play → Strategic Growth.

---

### Overlap 2 — New Revenue Model and Business Model Design
**Orchestrators in tension:** Revenue vs Strategic Growth

**The ambiguity:** Revenue claims "revenue model design." Strategic Growth claims "business model innovation."

**Resolution:**
- **Strategic Growth owns** business model design when the question is: *should we enter a new commercial model, a new market, or a new structural configuration of the business?* This is a strategic decision about what kind of business we are in.
- **Revenue owns** revenue model design when the question is: *how do we optimise the commercial mechanics of the model we have already committed to?* This is a commercial execution question.

**Rule:** New model vs new market → Strategic Growth leads. Optimise existing model → Revenue leads. When both, Strategic Growth leads the model decision; Revenue leads the commercial implementation once the model is set.

---

### Overlap 3 — Pricing
**Orchestrators in tension:** Revenue vs Founder's Office

**The ambiguity:** Revenue claims "pricing and packaging." Founder's Office claims pricing when it has "board or investor implications."

**Resolution:**
- **Revenue owns** pricing when the primary question is: *what price point and packaging maximises conversion and margin in our current market?*
- **Founder's Office owns** pricing when: *the pricing decision changes investor narrative, alters valuation logic, or signals a strategic repositioning of the business.*

**Rule:** Commercial pricing optimisation → Revenue. Pricing as strategic signal or investor-affecting decision → Founder's Office. When in doubt, Revenue leads and Founder's Office reviews.

---

### Overlap 4 — Upsell and Expansion
**Orchestrators in tension:** Client Experience vs Revenue

**The ambiguity:** CXO claims "expansion intelligence and timing." Revenue claims "upsell and expansion commercial motion."

**Resolution — split ownership is correct, but must be explicit:**
- **Client Experience owns** the intelligence layer: *is this client ready to expand? What is the health signal? What is the right timing and context?*
- **Revenue owns** the commercial layer: *how do we structure and execute the expansion conversation?*

**Handoff protocol:** CXO produces an expansion readiness brief (client health, timing recommendation, relationship context) and passes it to Revenue. Revenue does not initiate expansion conversations without CXO clearance on readiness. CXO does not execute the commercial motion.

**Rule:** No Revenue-led expansion without CXO expansion readiness signal. CXO does not override Revenue on commercial structure once timing is confirmed.

---

### Overlap 5 — Technical Infrastructure and DevOps
**Orchestrators in tension:** Product and Delivery vs Operational Reliability

**The ambiguity:** P&D claims technical architecture and infrastructure when it affects product outcomes. ORO claims infrastructure for operational systems.

**Resolution:**
- **P&D owns** infrastructure decisions when: *the decision directly determines what the product can or cannot do, how it performs for users, or when it affects deployment of product features.*
- **ORO owns** infrastructure decisions when: *the decision affects operational systems, business continuity, security posture, or internal tooling that supports business operations but is not part of the customer-facing product.*

**Rule:** Customer-facing or product-enabling infrastructure → P&D. Internal operational or business-continuity infrastructure → ORO. When a single infrastructure decision affects both (common in early-stage), P&D leads and ORO reviews for operational risk.

---

### Overlap 6 — Client Delivery Failures
**Orchestrators in tension:** Client Experience vs Operational Reliability

**The ambiguity:** When a delivery failure damages a client relationship, both orchestrators have a legitimate claim.

**Resolution — sequenced ownership:**
1. **CXO owns** the immediate response: trust repair, client communication, relationship management.
2. **ORO owns** the root cause diagnosis and structural fix: what operationally failed and how to prevent recurrence.

**Rule:** CXO acts first (client relationship is the immediate priority). ORO acts in parallel on root cause. CXO briefs ORO on the client impact. ORO briefs CXO on the operational fix timeline so CXO can communicate it to the client.

---

### Overlap 7 — M&A and Strategic Deals
**Orchestrators in tension:** Strategic Growth vs Founder's Office

**The ambiguity:** Strategic Growth claims M&A assessment. Founder's Office claims all high-stakes, irreversible founder-level decisions.

**Resolution:**
- **Strategic Growth owns** M&A when the question is: *is this target strategically sound, does the deal logic work, and can we integrate it?*
- **Founder's Office takes over** when the deal reaches: *capital commitment, board approval, investor communication, or a scale that changes the fundamental shape of the business.*

**Rule:** Strategic Growth leads M&A assessment through to a go/no-go recommendation. Founder's Office takes ownership at the moment of commitment decision and all external-facing deal communication.

---

### Overlap 8 — People Strategy and Culture
**Orchestrators in tension:** Operational Reliability vs Founder's Office

**The ambiguity:** ORO claims org structure and people operations. Founder's Office claims operating model decisions that affect the whole business.

**Resolution:**
- **ORO owns** people decisions that are operational: org structure design, role clarity, hiring sequencing, accountability systems, workforce planning.
- **Founder's Office owns** people decisions that are strategic: leadership team design, culture direction, values-level decisions, succession at the founder's direct reports level.

**Rule:** Execution-layer org design → ORO. Leadership team, culture, and founder-level succession → Founder's Office.

## Part 3 — Orchestrator Precedence Rules for Mixed Missions

When two orchestrators both have a valid claim to a mission, apply these precedence rules in order:

### Rule 1 — Founder-Level Precedence
If the mission involves capital commitment, irreversible decisions, investor/board communication, or the whole-business strategic identity, **Founder's Office takes precedence** over all other orchestrators.

*Exception: if the mission is clearly operational and below founder-level scope, ORO applies even if the founder is aware of it.*

### Rule 2 — Strategic-Before-Commercial Precedence
If the mission involves entering a new market, evaluating a new venture, or designing a new structural commercial model, **Strategic Growth takes precedence over Revenue** for the entry/design phase. Revenue takes over for commercial execution once the strategic commitment is made.

### Rule 3 — Relationship-Before-Commercial Precedence
In expansion and upsell situations, **Client Experience takes precedence over Revenue on timing**. Revenue cannot initiate an expansion conversation until CXO confirms expansion readiness. Once confirmed, Revenue takes precedence on the commercial execution.

### Rule 4 — Build-Before-Ops Precedence
When a technical infrastructure decision affects both product delivery and operational systems, **Product and Delivery takes precedence** for the decision. ORO participates as a reviewer for operational risk.

### Rule 5 — Repair-Before-Root-Cause Sequencing
When a delivery failure damages client relationships, **Client Experience acts first** on trust repair. ORO acts in parallel on root cause. Neither waits for the other, but the client relationship takes the immediate priority.

### Rule 6 — Execution-After-Strategy
No specialist orchestrator (Revenue, P&D, CX, ORO) leads a mission that requires a founder-level strategic decision as its first step. **Founder's Office frames before others execute.**

### Rule 7 — Single Owner Always
When precedence rules produce ambiguity, **Tess designates a single owner**. No mission proceeds with two orchestrators claiming equal ownership. Tess escalation is the tiebreaker.

## Part 4 — Orchestrator Selection Doctrine

This doctrine tells Tess when to use each routing option.

---

### When to Use Founder's Office as Owner

Use Founder's Office when **any** of the following are true:
- The decision affects the whole business's direction, identity, or capital structure
- The decision is irreversible and at a scale that changes what the business is
- The output will be seen by investors, the board, or major external stakeholders
- The decision requires the operator's personal authority, voice, or relationships
- The mission involves founder-mode work: strategic thinking, investor, operating model, negotiation at business level, executive messaging, or recovery with reputational implications
- No other orchestrator can credibly hold the full outcome

**Do not use Founder's Office** for missions that are clearly bounded within one operational or functional domain. Founder's Office is not a default — it is for the missions only the founder's office should touch.

---

### When to Use a Specialist Outcome Orchestrator as Owner

Use a specialist orchestrator when:
- The mission is clearly bounded within one outcome domain (revenue, product, client, growth, or operations)
- The mission does not require founder-level authority or cross-business coordination
- The outcome type maps cleanly to one orchestrator's primary outcomes
- The mission requires specialist guild coordination rather than whole-business synthesis

**Selection guide:**
- Commercial engine work → **Revenue**
- Build and ship work → **Product and Delivery**
- Client relationship work → **Client Experience**
- Expansion and market moves → **Strategic Growth**
- Operating system work → **Operational Reliability**

---

### When to Route Directly to a Single Guild Without a Full Outcome Orchestrator

Use direct guild routing when **all** of the following are true:
- The mission requires only one type of specialist expertise
- No cross-functional coordination is needed
- The task is execution, not decision
- The output is a specific deliverable, not a strategic recommendation
- Speed matters more than multi-guild synthesis

**Examples of direct guild routing:**
- "Draft the investor deck copy" → Messaging guild directly
- "Review this contract" → Legal guild directly
- "Model the unit economics for this scenario" → Finance guild directly
- "Build this API endpoint" → Coding guild directly
- "Research this competitor" → Research (Leah) directly

**Do not use direct guild routing** when the work affects a business-critical outcome, involves trade-offs that require synthesis, or when the output will drive a significant decision.

## Part 5 — Structural Blind Spots

The following gaps exist in the current agentic operating model. Each is assessed for severity and minimum recommended response.

---

### Blind Spot 1 — Brand as a Strategic Function
**Gap:** Brand is always a supporting guild. No orchestrator owns brand as a strategic business outcome — the differentiation, perception, and positioning of the business in its market over time.

**Impact:** Brand decisions that affect long-term competitive positioning may be made incidentally by Revenue (conversion-led), Founder's Office (messaging-led), or Strategic Growth (market-led) without coordinated strategic brand stewardship.

**Recommended response:** Assign brand strategy ownership explicitly to Founder's Office when it is strategic, and to Revenue when it is conversion-led. This covers most cases without a new orchestrator. Flag if a dedicated Brand Orchestrator becomes warranted as the business scales its brand investment significantly.

---

### Blind Spot 2 — Finance as a Strategic Function
**Gap:** Finance is always a supporting guild. No orchestrator owns financial performance, capital allocation, or financial strategy as a primary outcome.

**Impact:** Cross-cutting financial decisions — capital allocation across the business, financial model health, investment sequencing — may be undercoordinated.

**Recommended response:** Founder's Office owns capital allocation and financial strategy at business level. Revenue owns commercial financial performance. ORO owns operational cost structure. This covers the gap without a new orchestrator. Flag if the business reaches a scale where a CFO-function orchestrator is warranted.

---

### Blind Spot 3 — Data and Analytics as a Strategic Function
**Gap:** Analytics is always a supporting guild. No orchestrator owns data strategy, measurement infrastructure, or analytics capability as a primary outcome.

**Impact:** Building the analytics capability of the business — data infrastructure, measurement frameworks, decision intelligence systems — has no clear home.

**Recommended response:** Route analytics capability-building missions to P&D (if technical infrastructure) or ORO (if operational measurement design). This covers most cases. Flag if data strategy becomes a primary strategic initiative warranting dedicated orchestration.

---

### Blind Spot 4 — People and Culture as a Strategic Function
**Gap:** People sits primarily under ORO (operational layer) and Founder's Office (leadership layer). Culture as a strategic competitive asset — talent density, organisational character, employer brand — has no explicit orchestrator.

**Impact:** Culture transformation, talent strategy, and organisational effectiveness as strategic levers may be underweighted relative to their business impact.

**Recommended response:** Route culture and talent strategy missions to Founder's Office when they are strategic and leadership-level, and to ORO when they are operational and structural. This is sufficient for most stages. Flag if the business reaches a scale where a People and Culture Orchestrator is warranted.

---

### Blind Spot 5 — Legal and Compliance as a Primary Function
**Gap:** Legal is always a supporting guild. No orchestrator owns legal strategy, regulatory positioning, or compliance architecture as a primary outcome.

**Impact:** Businesses in regulated industries or with significant legal complexity may find that legal and compliance decisions are made reactively across multiple orchestrators rather than proactively as a strategic function.

**Recommended response:** Route legal strategy and compliance architecture to ORO (operational controls) and Founder's Office (strategic legal risk and board-level exposure). This covers the gap for most businesses. Flag if the business operates in a heavily regulated environment warranting a dedicated Legal and Compliance Orchestrator.

---

### Blind Spot 6 — Mission-to-Orchestrator Routing at Intake
**Gap:** The system currently has no explicit intake protocol that classifies an incoming mission before it is routed. Without this, Tess must infer routing from context — which creates inconsistency.

**Recommended response (minimum):** At every mission intake, Tess must ask three classification questions before any orchestrator is activated:
1. **Outcome type:** What outcome is being sought? (decide / design / build / convert / recover / govern / review / communicate / scale)
2. **Founder-level test:** Does this decision require founder-level authority, capital, or cross-business synthesis? If yes → Founder's Office.
3. **Domain test:** Does this mission map cleanly to one outcome domain? If yes → route to that orchestrator. If it spans multiple → apply precedence rules (Part 3).

This three-question intake should be made explicit in Tess's doctrine.

---

## Part 6 — Minimum Additional Layers Recommended

Based on the integration audit, the following are the only structural additions warranted at this stage:

### Recommended Addition 1 — Mission Intake Classification Protocol
**What:** A formal three-question intake protocol (see Blind Spot 6) that Tess applies before activating any orchestrator.  
**Why:** The most common routing failure will be skipping intake classification and defaulting to the most obvious orchestrator, rather than the correct one.  
**Effort:** Low — add to conductor/doctrine.md and conductor/guardrails.md.

### Recommended Addition 2 — Expansion Readiness Handoff Brief (Standard Format)
**What:** A standard brief format that CXO produces when signalling expansion readiness to Revenue.  
**Why:** The CXO → Revenue handoff on upsell/expansion is the highest-friction orchestrator boundary in the system. A standard brief prevents Revenue from acting without relationship context and prevents CXO from blocking commercial motion.  
**Effort:** Low — add to CXO doctrine as an appendix.

### No New Orchestrators Recommended at This Stage
The 6 current orchestrators cover the vast majority of real-world missions. Adding a 7th orchestrator before the current 6 are operational would add governance complexity faster than it adds capability. The blind spots identified above are manageable within the current architecture.

Re-evaluate when:
- Brand investment reaches a scale requiring dedicated strategic stewardship
- Finance complexity warrants a CFO-function orchestrator
- Data strategy becomes a primary strategic initiative
- The business enters a heavily regulated environment requiring dedicated legal/compliance orchestration
- People and culture becomes a primary strategic competitive lever requiring dedicated orchestration

## Part 7 — Integration Summary

The Outcome Orchestrator Layer is structurally sound with the following clarifications now in force:

| Boundary | Resolved Rule |
|---|---|
| FO vs Strategic Growth on positioning | FO = whole-business identity. SGO = market-specific positioning. |
| Revenue vs SGO on new models | SGO leads model decisions. Revenue leads model execution. |
| Revenue vs FO on pricing | Revenue = commercial optimisation. FO = investor/strategic signal. |
| CX vs Revenue on expansion | CX owns timing and readiness. Revenue owns commercial execution. |
| P&D vs ORO on infrastructure | P&D = customer-facing/product-enabling. ORO = internal/operational. |
| CX vs ORO on delivery failures | CX acts first (trust repair). ORO acts in parallel (root cause). |
| SGO vs FO on M&A | SGO leads assessment. FO takes over at commitment and external communication. |
| FO vs ORO on people | ORO = operational org. FO = leadership, culture, succession. |

Seven precedence rules govern mixed-mission ownership. A three-question intake protocol prevents routing errors at mission start. Direct guild routing is permitted for single-domain, execution-only, non-strategic work.
